Foundations of Quaternionic Quantum Computing:
Qubits, Spinors, and SU(2) Geometry

RQM Technologies Research Team
RQM Technologies
research@rqm-technologies.com

Abstract

We develop the foundational framework for quaternionic quantum computing, establishing rigorous connections between quaternion algebra, spinor representations, and the geometry of SU(2). We show that the quaternionic qubit—a unit quaternion in the Bloch sphere interpretation—provides a natural geometric encoding of two-level quantum systems, and that quaternion multiplication corresponds directly to SU(2) rotations on the Bloch sphere. We derive the quaternionic analogues of standard quantum gates, prove that any SU(2) gate can be expressed as a unit quaternion, and demonstrate that the double-cover relationship between SU(2) and SO(3) has direct implications for phase accumulation in quaternionic circuits. We further examine the quaternionic Hilbert space structure and discuss how non-commutativity of quaternion multiplication constrains the design of multi-qubit systems. This paper establishes the mathematical foundation for the subsequent papers in the RQM quaternionic quantum computing series.

1. Introduction

Quantum computing conventionally formulates quantum states as vectors in a complex Hilbert space, with unitary evolution described by complex matrix groups such as U(2n) for n-qubit systems. However, the underlying geometry of single-qubit dynamics is more naturally captured by the quaternion algebra 𝓭, whose unit elements form the group Sp(1) isomorphic to SU(2).

The identification of unit quaternions with SU(2) is classical—dating to Hamilton [1] and Cayley [2]—yet its implications for quantum computing have not been systematically developed as a unified computational framework. This paper fills that gap by:

  1. Giving precise definitions of the quaternionic qubit and its geometric interpretation;
  2. Proving the Sp(1)–SU(2) isomorphism and the double-cover of SO(3);
  3. Deriving quaternionic representations of standard single-qubit gates;
  4. Discussing the implications of non-commutativity for multi-qubit systems;
  5. Stating the foundational conjecture about the computational power of quaternionic circuits.

This paper is intended as the first in a series. All notation introduced here is used throughout subsequent papers. Readers are assumed familiar with basic linear algebra and quantum mechanics at the level of Nielsen and Chuang [10].

2. Quaternion Algebra

2.1 Definition and Basic Properties

Definition 1 — Quaternion Algebra

The quaternion algebra 𝓭 is the four-dimensional associative algebra over ℝ with basis {1, i, j, k} satisfying

i2 = j2 = k2 = ijk = −1.

Every quaternion q ∈ 𝓭 is written uniquely as q = a + bi + cj + dk with a, b, c, d ∈ ℝ. The real part of q is a; the remaining three components form the pure quaternion (or imaginary) part. The conjugate is q = a − bi − cj − dk, and the norm is |q| = √(a2 + b2 + c2 + d2).

The multiplication table for the basis units is:

×ijk
i−1k−j
j−k−1i
kj−i−1

Quaternion multiplication is associative but not commutative: ij = k but ji = −k. This non-commutativity is the defining algebraic feature exploited throughout this series.

2.2 The Quaternionic Qubit

Definition 2 — Quaternionic Qubit

A quaternionic qubit is a unit quaternion q ∈ 𝓭 with |q| = 1, representing a point on the 3-sphere S3 ⊂ ℝ4. The quaternionic qubit space is identified with SU(2) via the map

φ(a + bi + cj + dk) =
( a+di b+ci )
−b+ci a−di

3. SU(2) Geometry

3.1 The Quaternion–SU(2) Isomorphism

Theorem 1 — Quaternion–SU(2) Isomorphism

The group of unit quaternions Sp(1) = {q ∈ 𝓭 : |q| = 1} is isomorphic to SU(2) as a Lie group under the map φ of Definition 2.

The map φ is well-defined: for |q| = 1, the matrix φ(q) satisfies φ(q)†φ(q) = I and det(φ(q)) = a2 + b2 + c2 + d2 = |q|2 = 1, so φ(q) ∈ SU(2). The map is a group homomorphism since φ(q1q2) = φ(q1)φ(q2) (verified by direct matrix computation). Bijectivity follows from the fact that every SU(2) matrix U = [[α, β], [-β, α]] with |α|2 + |β|2 = 1 has a unique preimage under φ.

3.2 The Double Cover of SO(3)

Theorem 2 — Double Cover of SO(3)

The unit quaternion group Sp(1) is a double cover of SO(3): there exists a surjective Lie group homomorphism π: Sp(1) → SO(3) with ker(π) = {+1, −1}.

Define the adjoint action π(q)(v) = qvq−1 for pure quaternion v ∈ Im(𝓭) ≅ ℝ3. One checks: (i) π(q) preserves the inner product on Im(𝓭), (ii) det(π(q)) = +1, so π(q) ∈ SO(3), (iii) π is a group homomorphism, and (iv) ker(π) = {q : qvq−1 = v ∀ v ∈ Im(𝓭)} = {+1, −1}. Surjectivity follows from the fact that every rotation in SO(3) has a preimage in Sp(1).

Remark. Theorem 2 is the geometric origin of spinor phase: a 2π rotation in SO(3) corresponds to a path in Sp(1) from +1 to −1, not a loop. A quaternionic qubit therefore acquires a sign change (−1 global phase) under a 2π rotation, consistent with the spinor nature of spin-1/2 particles.

4. Quaternionic Quantum Gates

We now derive the quaternionic representations of the standard single-qubit gates.

Proposition 1 — Quaternionic Pauli Gates

Under the isomorphism φ of Theorem 1, right-multiplication of a quaternionic qubit by the basis quaternions i, j, k implements the Pauli gates X, Y, Z (up to global phase): the unit quaternions corresponding to X, Y, Z are i, j, k respectively.

More generally, a rotation by angle θ about unit axis n̂ = (nx, ny, nz) is represented by the unit quaternion

q(θ, n̂) = cos(θ/2) + sin(θ/2)(nxi + nyj + nzk).

4.2 Non-commutativity

Proposition 2 — Non-commutativity of Quaternionic Gates

For any two non-collinear rotation axes n̂1, n̂2 ∈ S2, the corresponding unit quaternion gates q1, q2 satisfy q1q2 ≠ q2q1 in general.

This is a direct consequence of the non-commutativity of 𝓭. It reflects the physical fact that rotations in 3D do not commute, and it is precisely this non-commutativity that enables universal quantum computation with a finite gate set.

5. Numerical Validation

Result 1 — Bloch Sphere Parametrization

Numerical experiments confirm that the quaternionic parametrization of the Bloch sphere via unit quaternions correctly reproduces all standard single-qubit gate operations to machine precision. See bloch_sphere_visualization.ipynb and quaternion_gates.py.

6. Outlook and Conjectures

Conjecture 1 — Quaternionic Multi-qubit Universality

A quaternionic quantum computing model that restricts entangling operations to quaternionic tensor products achieves universal quantum computation on a class of problems strictly larger than classical computation but potentially smaller than the full quantum circuit model.

The precise formulation of the quaternionic tensor product and the characterization of the computational class will be the subject of the next paper in this series (QQC-002).

7. Conclusion

We have established the foundational mathematical framework for quaternionic quantum computing:

  • The quaternion algebra 𝓭 and the quaternionic qubit as a unit quaternion in Sp(1);
  • The Sp(1)–SU(2) isomorphism and the double cover of SO(3);
  • Explicit quaternionic representations of standard single-qubit gates;
  • The non-commutativity of quaternion multiplication as the algebraic engine of gate universality;
  • Numerical validation of the Bloch sphere parametrization.

All notation and conventions introduced here are used throughout the RQM quaternionic quantum computing paper series.
