A Stable Right-Sided Quaternion Fourier Regime for Multichannel Spectral Analysis

A fixed-axis one-dimensional spectral seam for quaternion-lifted multichannel data.

John G. Van Geem
RQM Technologies
ORCID: 0009-0002-4003-8452

Abstract

This paper defines a stable right-sided discrete quaternion Fourier regime for multichannel spectral analysis. Building on a canonical quaternion lane representation of paired complex channels, it treats each lifted lane as a quaternion-valued sequence x[n] in H and adopts a fixed transform axis i. The forward transform is X[k] = Σn=0N-1 x[n] exp(-i2πnk/N), with inverse x[n] = (1/N) Σk=0N-1 X[k] exp(+i2πnk/N). The paper proves exact inversion, right-linearity over the commuting slice subalgebra Ci, nonnegativity of spectral power, and a Parseval identity Σn=0N-1|x[n]|2 = (1/N) Σk=0N-1|X[k]|2. These results justify the fixed-axis right-sided transform as a deterministic spectral seam for quaternion-lifted multichannel data.

1. Introduction

The first paper in this signal-processing series established a canonical quaternion lift for ordered multichannel RF and sensing captures. The present paper addresses the next layer: once a lane has the form x[n] in H, what Fourier convention should be treated as the stable public transform regime? Because quaternion Fourier analysis admits multiple legitimate conventions, this paper selects one narrow seam: one-dimensional, right-sided, fixed-axis, and lanewise.

2. Scope and signal model

A quaternion-valued lane is written as x = (x[n])n=0N-1, with x[n] in H. In the intended application these lanes arise after canonical quaternion lifting of paired complex channels, but this paper studies the spectral transform on one lane first before extending it lanewise to grouped multichannel captures.

3. Algebraic preliminaries

The transform kernel lives in the commuting slice Ci = {a + bi}, which is isomorphic to the ordinary complex numbers and contains the phase factors exp(iθ) = cos θ + i sin θ.

Definition 3.1 (Fixed transform slice). Ci is the commutative subalgebra {a + bi : a,b ∈ R} inside H.
Lemma 3.2 (Discrete kernel orthogonality). (1/N) Σk=0N-1 exp(i2π(m-n)k/N) = δmn.

4. Definition of the stable right-sided transform

Definition 4.1 (Forward transform). X[k] = Σn=0N-1 x[n] exp(-i2πnk/N), for k = 0,...,N-1.
Definition 4.2 (Inverse transform). x[n] = (1/N) Σk=0N-1 X[k] exp(+i2πnk/N), for n = 0,...,N-1.

The kernel is placed on the right. Because quaternion multiplication is noncommutative, this is a genuine part of the transform convention rather than a cosmetic choice.

5. Algebraic properties

Proposition 5.1 (Additivity). Fi{x+y}[k] = Fi{x}[k] + Fi{y}[k].
Proposition 5.2 (Right Ci-linearity). For a,b in Ci, Fi{xa+yb}[k] = Fi{x}[k]a + Fi{y}[k]b.

6. Exact inversion

Theorem 6.1 (Exact inversion). Fi-1(Fi{x})[m] = x[m] for all m = 0,...,N-1.

The proof substitutes the forward transform into the inverse and then collapses the inner exponential sum by discrete kernel orthogonality inside Ci.

7. Spectral power and Parseval invariance

Definition 7.1 (Spectral power). P[k] = |X[k]|2 = X[k]X[k]*.
Proposition 7.2. P[k] is real and nonnegative for every k.
Theorem 7.3 (Parseval identity). Σn=0N-1|x[n]|2 = (1/N) Σk=0N-1|X[k]|2.

8. Lanewise multichannel extension

For a grouped quaternion-lane capture Q[g,n], define Xg[k] = Fi{Q[g,·]}[k]. The single-lane Parseval identity extends immediately by summing over the lane index.

Proposition 8.1 (Lanewise multichannel Parseval identity). Σg,n|Q[g,n]|2 = (1/N) Σg,k|Xg[k]|2.

9. Why this convention is stable

The chosen transform regime is stable in an operational sense: axis choice, kernel placement, inverse convention, and normalization do not change across lanes or across downstream uses. This keeps implementation, testing, and interpretation aligned and makes the transform a clean spectral seam for later coherence metrics.

10. Scope, limitations, and non-claims

The paper does not claim universal superiority over other quaternion Fourier conventions, does not address left-sided, two-sided, higher-dimensional, or axis-adaptive transforms, and does not yet define the cross-lane coherence and orientation metrics that will build on the present spectral seam.

11. Conclusion

This paper establishes the spectral layer of the series. The first paper defined the canonical quaternion lift; the present paper defines the fixed-axis right-sided spectral transform on those lifted lanes. The next paper can therefore begin from spectra that are already deterministic, exactly invertible, and energy-controlled.
